CH 01

Today's Cybersecurity Analyst

Obj 1.5 · 2.1
Privacy vs. Security
PrivacyThe right of individuals to control how their personal information is collected, used, and shared. Focused on the individual's rights and expectations. SecurityThe protection of systems, data, and assets from unauthorized access or harm. Focused on organizational controls and technical safeguards. RelationshipSecurity is a prerequisite for privacy — you cannot have privacy without security. But security alone does not guarantee privacy. An organization can be technically secure while still violating privacy by misusing data it legally holds.
Generally Accepted Privacy Principles (GAPP)
1. ManagementThe organization documents its privacy practices in a privacy policy and related documents. 2. NoticeThe organization notifies individuals about its privacy practices — what it collects and how it is used. 3. Choice and ConsentThe organization obtains direct consent of individuals for the storage, use, and sharing of PII. 4. CollectionPII is collected only for the purposes identified in the notice and consented to by the individual. 5. Use, Retention, and DisposalInformation is only used for identified purposes. Information collected for one purpose may not be used for another nondisclosed purpose. 6. AccessIndividuals are provided access to any information the organization holds about them, upon request. 7. DisclosureInformation will be disclosed to third parties only when consistent with notice and consent. 8. SecurityPII will be protected against unauthorized access. 9. QualityThe organization will maintain accurate and complete information. 10. Monitoring and EnforcementBusiness processes are in place to ensure compliance with the privacy policy.
Risk Assessment (NIST SP 800-30)
ThreatAn outside force that may exploit a vulnerability. Can be adversarial, accidental, structural, or environmental. VulnerabilityA weakness that might allow an attack to take place. RiskThe combination of a threat and a corresponding vulnerability. Risk = Probability × Impact (magnitude). Adversarial ThreatsIndividuals, groups, and organizations deliberately trying to undermine security. Analysts assess capability, intent, and likelihood of targeting. Accidental ThreatsIndividuals doing routine work who mistakenly perform an action that undermines security. Consider the range of effects. Structural ThreatsEquipment, software, or environmental controls failing due to resource exhaustion or age. Consider the range of effects. Environmental ThreatsNatural or man-made disasters outside organizational control. Consider common threats to your geographic region. Qualitative AssessmentUses subjective ratings (Low/Medium/High) rather than financial figures. Results in a risk matrix. More practical than quantitative. Testable on exam. Quantitative AssessmentUses financial figures and statistical analysis (ALE = ARO × SLE). More precise but harder to implement. Not directly testable on CS0-003.
The exam explicitly notes: Qualitative = testable. Quantitative = not directly testable on CS0-003. Know the qualitative risk matrix — probability vs. magnitude grid where Risk = Probability × Magnitude.
Network Access Control (NAC)
NAC PurposeControls which devices can connect to the network. Checks device posture (AV status, patch level, encryption, domain membership) before granting access. Non-compliant devices are quarantined or given limited access. 802.1XThe IEEE standard for port-based network access control. Three roles: Supplicant (device trying to connect), Authenticator (switch or WAP), Authentication Server (RADIUS). Device cannot access the network until RADIUS approves it. RADIUSRemote Authentication Dial-In User Service. Authentication, Authorization, and Accounting (AAA) server. Used by 802.1X NAC deployments. Communicates between the authenticator and the identity/authentication store.
Firewall Types
Packet FilteringChecks packet characteristics (IP, port, protocol) against rules. No state tracking. Basic and fast. Found in routers and simple network devices. Easily bypassed by spoofing and fragmentation. Stateful InspectionTracks the state of network connections. Maintains a state table. Can determine if packets are part of a legitimate established session. Most basic stand-alone commercial firewalls. NGFW (Next-Generation Firewall)Incorporates application awareness, user identity, deep packet inspection, and built-in IPS. Current state-of-the-art for network perimeter protection. WAF (Web Application Firewall)Specialized firewall protecting against web application attacks — SQLi, XSS, CSRF. Operates at Layer 7. Understands HTTP/HTTPS traffic. Triple-Homed FirewallFirewall with three network interfaces — internal, external (internet), and DMZ. Allows traffic to the DMZ from the internet without exposing the internal network.
Honeypots, DNS Sinkholes & Network Segmentation
HoneypotDecoy system designed to attract attackers. Appears to be a legitimate, valuable target. Heavily monitored — captures attacker TTPs, tools, and behaviors. Used to feed threat intelligence and network blacklists. Isolated from production systems. HoneynetA network of honeypots that simulates an entire enterprise environment to attract and study sophisticated attackers. DNS SinkholeDetects suspicious DNS requests (typically from botnet-infected systems reaching out to C2 servers) and responds with the IP address of a sinkhole system. The sinkhole detects and can remediate or isolate infected systems. Effective for identifying compromised hosts. Network SegmentationDivides a network into zones to limit lateral movement and contain breaches. High-value assets (servers, databases) should be in separate segments from user workstations. VLANs, subnets, and firewalls enforce segmentation.
Endpoint Security & Penetration Testing
Endpoint HardeningReducing the attack surface of individual devices. Common practices: patch management, remove unnecessary software, restrict admin rights, enable full-disk encryption, deploy host-based firewall, configure application whitelisting, and use EDR. Group Policy (GPO)Windows Active Directory mechanism to enforce security settings across many computers simultaneously. Can enforce firewall rules, software restrictions, password policies, and more. Pen Test — Planning PhaseAdministrative groundwork. No technical work performed. Three key rules of engagement: Timing (when and will staff be notified?), Scope (what is in/out of scope?), Authorization (who is authorizing it? What happens if confronted?). Pen Test — Attack PhaseActive exploitation of identified vulnerabilities. Use only techniques and tools within the agreed scope. Document all actions taken for the final report. Reverse EngineeringAnalyzing software or hardware without access to source code or design documentation. Used to discover vulnerabilities, analyze malware, and understand undocumented system behaviors. Classified as static analysis technique in vulnerability scanning context.
Machine Learning & Automation in Security
ML in SecurityAutomatically extracts knowledge from large volumes of security data. Reduces requirements for human analysts to manually sift through alerts. Guides analysts toward productive work by surfacing high-probability threats. Used in UEBA (User and Entity Behavior Analytics), SIEM correlation, and EDR. Efficiency PrinciplesStandardize processes: identify tasks suitable for automation (repeatable, no human interaction needed), coordinate team to facilitate automation. Streamline operations: use SOAR, orchestrate threat intelligence data enrichment, minimize human engagement in routine tasks. Single Pane of GlassConsolidated security dashboard showing all security tool data in one interface. Reduces alert fatigue and enables faster correlation. Typically implemented via SIEM. Objective 1.5 explicitly tests this concept. Tool IntegrationAPIs (Application Programming Interfaces) allow different security tools to share data programmatically. Webhooks send event-driven notifications. Plugins extend tool functionality.

System and Network Architecture

Obj 1.1
Cloud Service Models
ModelWhat Customer ManagesWhat Provider ManagesExamples
SaaSData, user accessEverything else — app, platform, infrastructureOffice 365, Salesforce, Gmail
PaaSApplications, dataRuntime, middleware, OS, virtualization, hardwareAWS Elastic Beanstalk, Azure App Service

Cloud Deployment Models
Public CloudInfrastructure provisioned for any customer in a multitenant environment. Managed and operated by a CSP. Lowest cost but least control. Private CloudProvisioned for use by a single organization. May be on-premises or hosted by a provider. Highest control and customization. Community CloudMultitenant but tenants are limited to members of a specific community (e.g., government agencies, healthcare organizations). Hybrid CloudBlends public, private, and/or community cloud services. Data and applications can move between environments based on need.
Shared Responsibility Model

Security responsibilities are split between the cloud provider and the customer. The division shifts depending on the service model. The customer is always responsible for their data and access management regardless of service model. The provider is always responsible for physical infrastructure security. Everything in between depends on whether it's IaaS, PaaS, or SaaS.

Misconfiguration is the #1 cause of cloud breaches — and under the shared responsibility model, configuration is almost always the customer's responsibility. If you misconfigure an S3 bucket or leave a security group open, that's on you, not AWS.
Modern Network Architecture
Zero TrustNever trust, always verify. No implicit trust based on network location. Every user, device, and request must be authenticated and authorized regardless of whether they are inside or outside the perimeter. Uses microsegmentation, least privilege, and continuous validation. SASE (Secure Access Service Edge)Converges SD-WAN with cloud-delivered security services. Combines CASB, Zero Trust network access (ZTNA), SWG (Secure Web Gateway), FWaaS (Firewall as a Service), and more into a single cloud-native platform. Identity-centric rather than perimeter-centric. SDN (Software-Defined Networking)Relies on software/code to define and manage the network. Decouples the control plane from the data plane. Centralized controller manages all traffic decisions. Enables programmable, consistent network policy enforcement. Network SegmentationDivides the network into distinct zones to limit lateral movement. Uses VLANs, subnets, and firewalls. DMZ isolates public-facing services. Microsegmentation applies policies at the individual workload level. On-PremisesInfrastructure physically located and managed within the organization's own facilities. Full control but highest cost and maintenance burden. Hybrid ArchitectureMix of on-premises and cloud resources. Common in enterprises transitioning to cloud. Introduces complexity in security policy enforcement and monitoring.
Infrastructure as Code (IaC)
IaC DefinitionProvisioning and managing infrastructure through machine-readable configuration files rather than manual processes. Examples: Terraform, CloudFormation, Ansible. Security BenefitsIncreases reusability of code. Increases speed of infrastructure creation. Reduces likelihood of configuration errors by leveraging common templates. Creates an auditable, version-controlled record of infrastructure state. Security RisksHardcoded credentials in IaC files. Misconfigured templates replicated at scale. Secrets stored in version control. Use secrets managers, not IaC files, for sensitive credentials.
Identity and Access Management
MFAMulti-Factor Authentication. Requires two or more factors from different categories: something you know, something you have, something you are. Significantly reduces credential-based attacks. TOTP apps more secure than SMS. SSOSingle Sign-On. One authentication event grants access to multiple systems. Reduces password fatigue and credential sprawl. Kerberos implements SSO in Active Directory. SAML and OAuth enable federated SSO across organizations. FederationExtends SSO across organizational boundaries. Enables "login with Google/Microsoft/GitHub." Identity Provider (IdP) authenticates; Service Provider (SP) trusts that assertion. Key technologies: SAML, OAuth 2.0, OpenID Connect. PAM (Privileged Access Management)Technologies and practices managing and securing privileged accounts. Relies on least privilege. Addresses over-provisioning, privilege creep (retaining access as roles change), embedded credentials, and similar issues. Includes password vaulting, JIT access, and PAWs (Privileged Access Workstations). PasswordlessAuthentication without a password. Uses a single factor designed to be more secure than a password — USB token, authenticator app, biometric. Unlike MFA, relies on one more-secure factor rather than two weaker ones. CASBCloud Access Security Broker. Policy enforcement points between users and cloud applications. Provides visibility, data security, threat protection, and compliance. Inline CASB sits in the traffic path. API-based CASB connects via cloud APIs.
OS Concepts and System Hardening
Windows RegistryDatabase containing Windows system settings. Key root keys: HKEY_LOCAL_MACHINE (HKLM) — system info, scheduled tasks, services. HKEY_CURRENT_USER (HKCU) — logged-in user settings. HKEY_CLASSES_ROOT (HKCR) — COM registrations. HKEY_USERS — all user accounts. HKEY_CURRENT_CONFIG — deprecated hardware info. System HardeningReducing the attack surface of systems. Common practices: update and patch, remove unnecessary software and services, restrict/log administrative access, control account creation, enable logging and monitoring, use disk encryption and Secure Boot. File Structure / Config LocationsWindows: settings in Registry, C:\ProgramData\, C:\Program Files\, and user AppData. Linux: /etc/ for system-wide config. macOS: ~/Library/Preferences and /Library/Preferences. Hardware Architecture ImpactMost servers use x86 (Intel/AMD). Growing number of ARM-based systems. Malware compiled for x86 will not run on ARM. This means some threats are architecture-specific — important consideration in threat analysis. Log IngestionTime synchronization (NTP) is critical — logs from different systems must have synchronized timestamps to enable correlation. Logging levels determine how much is captured — verbose logging provides more detail but increases storage and processing requirements.
Infrastructure Concepts: Serverless, VMs, Containers
Serverless (FaaS)Function as a Service. Code runs in stateless, ephemeral functions. Provider manages all infrastructure. Examples: AWS Lambda, Azure Functions, Google App Engine. Security shifts to function code, IAM permissions, and event triggers. VirtualizationMultiple VMs running on shared hardware, each with its own OS. Strong isolation. Hypervisor manages resource allocation. Vulnerabilities: VM escape, hypervisor attacks, noisy neighbor issues. ContainerizationApplication-level virtualization. Containers package apps and dependencies. Share the host OS kernel — no guest OS. Faster and lighter than VMs. Weaker isolation — kernel compromise affects all containers. Docker, Kubernetes.
Encryption and Data Protection
PKIPublic Key Infrastructure. System of CAs, certificates, and processes for managing asymmetric key pairs. Enables HTTPS, email signing/encryption, code signing, and mutual TLS. Certificate lifecycle: enrollment → issue → use → expire/revoke. SSL Inspection (TLS Inspection)Process by which security devices (NGFWs, proxies) decrypt, inspect, and re-encrypt TLS traffic to detect threats hidden inside HTTPS. Requires installing the inspection device's CA certificate on client devices. Raises privacy concerns. DLP (Data Loss Prevention)Monitors and controls data movement to prevent unauthorized exfiltration. Can block emails, file transfers, or USB copies of sensitive data. Network DLP inspects traffic; endpoint DLP monitors local actions. PIIPersonally Identifiable Information. Any data that can identify an individual. Protected by GDPR, CCPA, HIPAA, and other regulations. Examples: name, SSN, email, IP address, biometrics. CHD (Cardholder Data)Credit/debit card data (PAN, cardholder name, expiration, CVV). Regulated by PCI DSS. Must be protected and minimized.
CH 03

Malicious Activity

Obj 1.2 · 1.3
Network Indicators of Malicious Activity
BeaconingInfected host periodically sends traffic to a C2 (Command and Control) server. Regular, predictable intervals. Often HTTP/HTTPS to blend with normal traffic. Can request commands, provide status, or download additional malware. Detected via IDS/IPS rules, traffic analysis, and anomaly detection. Bandwidth ConsumptionUnusually high traffic volume. May indicate data exfiltration, DDoS participation, or malware activity. Detected using flow data (PRTG, NetFlow), SNMP monitoring, and threshold alerting. Irregular Peer-to-PeerTraffic between systems that don't normally communicate directly. May indicate lateral movement, botnet communication, or unauthorized data sharing. Rogue DevicesUnauthorized devices on the network — rogue APs, unauthorized laptops, unknown IoT devices. Detected with network scanning, NAC, and wireless monitoring. Scans/SweepsICMP sweeps, port scans, service enumeration. Reconnaissance activity. Often precedes an attack. Detected via IDS/IPS and log analysis. Unusual Traffic SpikesSudden increases in traffic volume or connection count. Could indicate DoS attack, exfiltration, or malware spreading. Activity on Unexpected PortsServices running on non-standard ports. Malware often communicates on unusual ports to evade detection, or hijacks legitimate ports for C2.
Host Indicators of Malicious Activity
Processor/Memory ConsumptionUnexpectedly high CPU or RAM usage. Malware (cryptomining, botnets) often consumes significant resources. Monitor with top, ps, Task Manager, Resource Monitor, Performance Monitor. Drive CapacityUnexpected disk usage growth. Malware may cache data, store payloads, or fill disks as a DoS tactic. Unauthorized SoftwareApplications installed without authorization. Detected via software inventory, application whitelisting, and MDM. Malicious ProcessesRunning processes not associated with known legitimate software. Hidden processes (rootkits). Processes with names designed to mimic legitimate system processes. Unauthorized ChangesModifications to system files, configuration, or scheduled tasks not initiated by authorized change management. Unauthorized PrivilegesAccount privileges escalated beyond their authorization. New admin accounts created. Group memberships modified. Data ExfiltrationLarge volumes of data being transferred outbound. Sensitive files being accessed at unusual times or volumes. Detected via DLP, SIEM, and NetFlow analysis. File System Changes/AnomaliesNew files appearing in unusual locations. System file modifications. Encryption of files (ransomware indicator). Detected with file integrity monitoring (FIM) and SIEM. Registry ChangesPersistence mechanisms often added to registry run keys: HKLM\Software\Microsoft\Windows\CurrentVersion\Run and HKCU equivalents. Unauthorized changes indicate potential compromise. Unauthorized Scheduled TasksMalware commonly creates scheduled tasks for persistence or to execute payloads periodically. Review schtasks (Windows) or crontab (Linux) for unauthorized entries.
DoS & DDoS
DoS PatternsOverwhelming a network or service with volume (volumetric). Attacking specific vulnerabilities to cause failure (protocol or application layer). Attacking intermediary systems to block traffic between two locations. DDoSDistributed — attacks originate from many systems/networks simultaneously. Harder to detect the source. Harder to stop. Often uses botnets of compromised devices. Detection: traffic analysis, rate limiting, anomaly detection. Cloud-Based DDoS MitigationScrubbing centers absorb and filter attack traffic before it reaches the organization. Examples: Cloudflare, Akamai, AWS Shield. Traffic is routed through the mitigation service during an attack.
Detection Tools
SIEMSecurity Information and Event Management. Centralizes log collection from all sources. Applies correlation rules to identify patterns across disparate events. Generates alerts. Provides dashboards and reports. Examples: Splunk, Graylog, ELK Stack, IBM QRadar. SOARSecurity Orchestration, Automation, and Response. Integrates security tools via APIs. Executes playbooks automatically when specific triggers occur. Automates routine response tasks. Reduces mean time to respond (MTTR). Incident management and reporting built in. EDREndpoint Detection and Response. Agent deployed on endpoints. Focuses on threat behavior patterns. Automatically responds to neutralize threats or notify admins. Data used in incident response. Examples: CrowdStrike, Carbon Black, Microsoft Defender for Endpoint. XDRExtended Detection and Response. Extends EDR beyond endpoints to include network, cloud, email, and identity data. Unified detection and response across the entire attack surface. Next evolution beyond EDR. WiresharkGUI packet analyzer. Deep protocol dissection. Opens .pcap files. Filter syntax: ip.addr == 192.168.1.1, tcp.port == 443, http. Essential for manual traffic analysis. tcpdumpCLI packet capture. Most common packet capture tool on Linux servers. tcpdump -i eth0 port 53 captures DNS. -w file.pcap saves for Wireshark analysis. WHOISQueries domain registration and ownership information. Useful for attribution and identifying malicious domains. AbuseIPDBIP and domain reputation service. Lists IPs involved in malicious activities. Check suspicious source IPs against this database during incident response. VirusTotalSubmit files, hashes, URLs for multi-engine malware analysis. Aggregates results from 70+ AV engines. Used for file analysis and malware identification. StringsCommand-line tool that extracts printable strings from binary files. Quick analysis without executing the file. Can reveal embedded URLs, file paths, registry keys, and other indicators. Joe Sandbox / CuckooAutomated malware sandboxes. Execute suspicious files in an isolated, heavily instrumented environment. Capture all system calls, network traffic, file changes, and registry modifications. Safe analysis of potentially destructive malware.
Email Analysis
Email Header AnalysisHeaders reveal the true path an email traveled. Check: Received-from chain (tracing actual sending servers), X-Originating-IP, Return-Path vs From address. Headers can be forged but the Received chain is harder to fake. SPF (Sender Policy Framework)DNS TXT record listing authorized mail servers for a domain. Receiving server checks if the sending IP is on the list. Prevents spoofing of the MAIL FROM address. DKIM (DomainKeys Identified Mail)Adds a cryptographic digital signature to email headers. Receiving server verifies signature using the sender's public key published in DNS. Proves the email was actually sent by the domain and was not tampered with in transit. DMARCDomain-based Message Authentication, Reporting, and Conformance. Builds on SPF and DKIM. Specifies what to do if messages fail SPF/DKIM checks (none, quarantine, reject). Provides reporting back to the domain owner. Essential for protecting against email spoofing. Impersonation / PhishingEmail appearing to be from a trusted source. Look for: display name vs actual email address mismatch, slightly misspelled domains (typosquatting), obfuscated or shortened URLs, urgency language, unexpected attachments. Embedded LinksHover over links to see actual destination before clicking. Obfuscated URLs redirect to malicious sites. QR codes in emails can bypass URL scanning.
SPF, DKIM, and DMARC work together. SPF = authorized sending IPs. DKIM = cryptographic signature proving the domain sent it. DMARC = policy for what to do when SPF or DKIM fail AND reporting. All three are needed for comprehensive email authentication. Know what each one protects against.
Network Monitoring Methods
Active MonitoringSends traffic to test or measure network behavior. Examples: ICMP ping, iPerf bandwidth testing. Adds traffic to the network. Provides real-time measurements. Can establish performance baselines. Passive MonitoringCaptures and analyzes traffic without adding traffic to the network. Provides a detailed view of actual traffic content and behavior. After-the-fact analysis since packets must be captured first. Examples: tcpdump, Wireshark, IDS in promiscuous mode. Router-Based MonitoringUses NetFlow, RMON, or SNMP to collect traffic statistics from routers and switches. NetFlow provides flow-level metadata (IP pairs, ports, volumes) without capturing packet contents. Lower overhead than full packet capture. NetFlow / IPFIXNetwork traffic metadata — source/dest IP, port, protocol, byte count, packet count, timestamps. Does NOT capture payload. Used to detect anomalies and large data transfers without full packet capture overhead. SNMPSimple Network Management Protocol. Polls network devices for interface statistics, error counts, CPU/memory utilization. Port 161 (queries), 162 (traps). Used for bandwidth monitoring and device health alerts.
CH 04

Threat Intelligence

Obj 1.4
The Intelligence Cycle
Requirements Gathering. Identify what intelligence is needed. Assess past breaches, industry trends, and risk assessments. Determine what information could have prevented or limited past incidents.
Data Collection. Gather data from threat intelligence sources to meet requirements. May repeat as requirements are refined.
Processing and Analysis. Convert raw data into usable formats. Feed into automated tools or write reports. Analyze for relevance, accuracy, and actionability.
Dissemination. Distribute intelligence to leadership and operational personnel who will act on it.
Feedback. Gather feedback on the quality and usefulness of the intelligence produced. Use to improve requirements and the overall process. Continuous improvement is critical.
Intelligence Confidence Levels
TimelinessHow current is the intelligence? Older intelligence about threat actor TTPs may still be relevant if behavior hasn't changed, but information about active C2 infrastructure becomes stale quickly. RelevancyDoes this intelligence apply to your organization, industry, or geography? Threat intelligence about attacks targeting industrial control systems in energy companies may not be relevant to a retail organization. AccuracyHow reliable is the source? Has the information been corroborated by multiple independent sources? Consider the source's track record and methodology.
The exam expects you to assess threat intelligence based on these three factors: timeliness, relevancy, and accuracy. Know why each matters. A piece of intelligence can be accurate but not timely (outdated), or timely but not relevant (different industry sector).
Threat Actors
APT (Advanced Persistent Threat)Sophisticated, well-resourced threat actor with long-term objectives. Typically nation-state or state-sponsored. Establishes persistent access and maintains it for months or years. Prioritizes stealth over speed. Examples: APT28 (Fancy Bear), Lazarus Group. Nation-StateGovernment-sponsored hackers. Highest capability and resources. Objectives: espionage, sabotage of critical infrastructure, financial theft. Most sophisticated TTPs. Organized CrimeFinancially motivated criminal organizations. Common activities: ransomware, financial fraud, BEC (Business Email Compromise), credential theft. Often operate ransomware-as-a-service (RaaS) models. HacktivistsMotivated by ideology or political causes rather than financial gain. Use cyberattacks to promote their agenda. Tactics: DDoS, defacement, data leaks. Script KiddieLow-skill attacker using pre-built tools and scripts without understanding how they work. Opportunistic. Targets easy vulnerabilities, not specific organizations. Insider ThreatMalicious insider: deliberately undermines security for personal gain, revenge, or ideology. Unintentional insider: accidentally causes security incidents through negligence, social engineering, or mistakes. Both are significant risks. Detected via UEBA. Supply ChainThreat entering through a third-party vendor, software update, or hardware component. Compromising one supplier can provide access to thousands of targets. Examples: SolarWinds, XZ Utils backdoor, NotPetya (MeDoc software update).
TTPs — Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures
TTPsThe behavioral fingerprint of a threat actor. Tactics = high-level goals (what they want to achieve). Techniques = general methods used. Procedures = specific implementations of techniques. TTPs are used to identify, attribute, and track threat actors across incidents. MITRE ATT&CKComprehensive knowledge base of adversary tactics and techniques based on real-world observations. Organized into a matrix. Tactics are columns (Initial Access, Execution, Persistence, etc.). Techniques are rows within each tactic. Used for threat modeling, detection engineering, and red/blue team planning.
Intelligence Collection Sources

Open Source (OSINT)

  • Social media — threat actor activity, leaked data, reconnaissance
  • Blogs and forums — vulnerability disclosures, PoC code, TTPs
  • Government bulletins — CISA advisories, FBI notifications, DHS alerts
  • CERT/CSIRT — Computer Emergency Response Teams publish advisories on active threats and vulnerabilities
  • Deep/Dark web — monitoring for leaked credentials, sale of exploits, threat actor communications

Closed Source

  • Paid threat intelligence feeds — Recorded Future, Mandiant, CrowdStrike Falcon Intelligence. Higher quality, faster reporting, more actionable
  • Information sharing organizations — ISACs (Information Sharing and Analysis Centers) for specific sectors (FS-ISAC, H-ISAC, E-ISAC). Share threat intelligence within trusted communities
  • Internal sources — your own logs, incident history, honeypot data, SOC observations. Most relevant to your specific environment
Uses of Threat Intelligence
Incident ResponseIdentify attackers and their TTPs during active incidents. Map observed behaviors to known threat actors. Guide containment and eradication strategies based on known actor behavior. Vulnerability ManagementThreat intelligence influences risk ratings. Vulnerabilities being actively exploited by known threat actors targeting your industry should be prioritized. Risk ManagementInform risk assessments with up-to-date knowledge of the threat landscape. Change risk ratings as the threat environment evolves. Security EngineeringInfluence architectural designs based on threat models. Build defenses specifically against the TTPs used by likely threat actors. Detection and MonitoringUse IoCs from threat intel feeds to build detection rules in SIEM and IDS/IPS. Block known malicious IPs, domains, and hashes at the perimeter.
Threat Hunting
Threat HuntingProactive, human-driven search for threats that have evaded automated detection. Starts with a hypothesis based on threat intelligence or anomalous data. The hunter queries available data sources to prove or disprove the hypothesis. Different from passive monitoring — it's active searching. Focus AreasConfigurations and misconfigurations (attackers frequently exploit these). Isolated or secured networks (unexpected activity here is high-priority). Business-critical assets and processes (highest value targets). Active DefenseResponding to attacks as they occur. May involve deception — tarpits slow attackers and provide false data. Honeypots attract and monitor attackers. Active defense is more aggressive than purely reactive monitoring. IoC Collection, Analysis, ApplicationCollection: gather data from tools, logs, and feeds. Analysis: determine if gathered data actually indicates compromise (context matters — unusual traffic may be legitimate). Application: trigger incident response when confirmed, or use as detection signatures going forward.
CH 05

Reconnaissance & Intelligence Gathering

Obj 2.1 · 2.2
Passive Reconnaissance
Passive ReconnaissanceGathering information without directly interacting with the target system. Uses publicly available data. Undetectable by the target. Examples: WHOIS lookups, DNS queries, reviewing public websites, job postings, social media, and Shodan. Log and Configuration AnalysisIf access to local system logs is available, they provide a treasure trove of information — user accounts, system configurations, network topology, application details, and security settings. Logs reveal how systems interact and what vulnerabilities may exist. OSINT SourcesGoogle/search engines (site: and filetype: operators). Shodan (internet-connected device search engine). LinkedIn/social media (employee names, technologies). DNS records (MX, A, AAAA, TXT reveal infrastructure). Certificate Transparency logs (discover subdomains).
Active Reconnaissance Tools
nmapMost commonly used CLI port scanner. Free and open source. Capabilities: multiple scan modes (SYN, TCP connect, UDP, XMAS), OS fingerprinting, service/version detection, scripting engine (NSE). Basic usage: nmap target. Service detection: nmap -sV target. OS detection: nmap -O target. Angry IP ScannerCross-platform GUI port scanner (Windows/Linux/macOS). Requires Java. Supports pluggable fetcher modules for additional data collection. Configure target ports under Preferences before scanning — no ports configured = no port data returned. MaltegoOpen-source intelligence gathering and link analysis tool. GUI-based. Builds relationship maps connecting people, domains, IPs, organizations, and other entities. Uses "transforms" to discover connections. Good for mapping an organization's external attack surface. Metasploit Framework (MSF)Penetration testing framework. Open source and commercial versions. Capabilities: port scanning (tcp, syn, wmap), service enumeration, vulnerability exploitation, payload delivery. In example: demonstrates SSH version detection capability. Recon-ngWeb-based OSINT reconnaissance tool. Often paired with Kali Linux. Reduces time spent manually gathering from open resources. Modular framework with many modules for different data sources. Aggregates OSINT into a structured database. hpingAdvanced version of ping. Allows customization of echo requests. Can craft custom packets for more aggressive reconnaissance. Used to bypass some firewall rules and test specific network paths. ZenmapGUI front-end for nmap. Provides topology visualization of scanned networks — useful for understanding network layout and identifying devices that don't belong.
Port Scanning Concepts
Well-Known PortsPorts 0–1023. Assigned to specific services by IANA. Examples: 22 (SSH), 80 (HTTP), 443 (HTTPS), 25 (SMTP), 53 (DNS), 3389 (RDP). Registered PortsPorts 1024–49151. Assigned by IANA on request for specific services. Examples: 3306 (MySQL), 5432 (PostgreSQL), 8080 (HTTP alternate), 27017 (MongoDB). Dynamic/Private PortsPorts 49152–65535. Used as ephemeral (temporary) source ports by client applications. Service/Version IdentificationScanners can identify what service and version is running on a port by analyzing the response (banner grabbing). Knowing the exact version allows cross-referencing against vulnerability databases. OS FingerprintingAnalyzing TCP/IP stack behavior to identify the operating system. Based on subtle differences in how different OS implementations handle network packets. Useful for identifying targets and likely vulnerabilities. EnumerationDetailed information gathering after initial discovery. Gathers: OS details, network topology, valid usernames, machine names, share names, directory structures, web server details. Used to identify specific attack vectors.

Vulnerability Scanning Types
Active ScanningDirectly interacts with scanned hosts. Sends probes and analyzes responses. More thorough detection of vulnerabilities. Disadvantages: generates detectable network traffic, can cause performance issues, may disrupt sensitive systems. Passive ScanningMonitors network traffic without sending probes. Like IDS monitoring. Advantages: invisible to targets, no performance impact. Disadvantages: only detects vulnerabilities reflected in network traffic — misses many issues. Credentialed ScanningScanner authenticates to target systems and retrieves configuration data from the inside. More accurate than non-credentialed — can check actual installed versions, registry settings, and file permissions. Fewer false positives. Can access OS, databases, and applications. Non-Credentialed ScanningTests systems from the outside without authentication. Simulates attacker perspective. Less accurate — more false positives. Cannot assess internal configuration. Good for testing what attackers can see. Agent-BasedSoftware agent installed on target systems. Provides continuous monitoring and assessment. Works even when the system is off-network (laptops). No network exposure of credentials. AgentlessScans over the network without installed agents. Easier to deploy at scale. Depends on network connectivity and credentials. May miss offline or isolated systems. Internal ScanningConducted from inside the network. Reveals what an attacker with internal access would see. Detects issues not visible from outside the firewall. External ScanningConducted from outside the network. Reveals what an external attacker sees. Tests perimeter defenses and internet-exposed services. Static AnalysisAnalyzes software code or binary without executing it. Identifies vulnerabilities in source code. Part of SAST tools in SDLC. Dynamic AnalysisTests running applications. Includes reverse engineering (analyzing software without source code) and fuzzing (sending malformed/random input to find crashes).
The exam expects you to know all these scanning types and when to use each: credentialed vs. non-credentialed, agent vs. agentless, internal vs. external, active vs. passive, static vs. dynamic. Be prepared for scenario questions asking which scan type is most appropriate for a given situation.
Scanning Considerations
SchedulingScans should be scheduled to minimize impact on production systems. Run during off-peak hours. Coordinate with system owners. PCI DSS requires quarterly scans plus scans after significant changes. Sensitivity LevelsSafe scans minimize the risk of disrupting systems. More aggressive scans may trigger IDS/IPS or cause instability on fragile systems. Adjust sensitivity based on the criticality of target systems. SegmentationScans should cover all network segments. Firewalls between segments may block scan traffic. Credentialed scans may require separate credentials per segment. Consider scan perspective from each segment. Regulatory RequirementsPCI DSS requires quarterly internal and external scans. FISMA requires scans per NIST SP 800-53. Regulatory requirements may dictate scan frequency, scope, and vendor qualification requirements. Critical InfrastructureOT (Operational Technology), ICS (Industrial Control Systems), and SCADA systems require special handling. Aggressive scans can disrupt or physically damage these systems. Use passive scanning or carefully configured safe-mode active scanning. Plugin ManagementEach plugin checks for a specific vulnerability. Disable unnecessary plugins to improve scan speed and reduce false positives. Update plugins daily to ensure coverage of newly discovered vulnerabilities.
Vulnerability Management Lifecycle
Asset Discovery. Identify all systems in scope. Use network scanners to find previously unknown systems. Build and maintain an asset inventory.
Vulnerability Scanning. Conduct scans per schedule and policy. Use appropriate scan type (credentialed, internal/external, agent-based as needed).
Prioritization. Score vulnerabilities using CVSS. Factor in asset value, exploitability, exposure, and threat intelligence. Create remediation priority queue.
Remediation. Apply patches, configuration changes, or compensating controls. Track remediation through to completion. Coordinate with system owners.
Verification. Re-scan after remediation to confirm vulnerabilities are resolved. Validate that patches were successfully applied.
Reporting and Documentation. Report results to stakeholders. Document exceptions and accepted risks. Update asset and vulnerability inventories.

Compliance Frameworks
PCI DSSPayment Card Industry Data Security Standard. Mandatory for merchants handling credit card transactions. Key scanning requirements: quarterly internal and external scans (requirement 11.2), scans after significant changes, use of approved scanning vendors (ASV) for external scans, qualified personnel for internal scans. CVSS — Common Vulnerability Scoring System

CVSS provides a standardized numeric score (0–10) representing the severity of a vulnerability. Higher = more severe. Scores: 0.0=None, 0.1–3.9=Low, 4.0–6.9=Medium, 7.0–8.9=High, 9.0–10.0=Critical.

MetricOptionsWhat It Measures
Attack Vector (AV)Network (N), Adjacent (A), Local (L), Physical (P)How the attacker exploits the vulnerability. Network = remotely exploitable = highest score.
Attack Complexity (AC)Low (L), High (H)Conditions beyond attacker control. Low = straightforward exploit.
Privileges Required (PR)None (N), Low (L), High (H)Level of access attacker needs before exploiting. None = no auth required = higher score.
User Interaction (UI)None (N), Required (R)Whether another person must take action. None = no user needed = higher score.
Scope (S)Unchanged (U), Changed (C)Does the vulnerability affect components beyond its authorization scope? Changed = can impact other systems.
Confidentiality (C)None (N), Low (L), High (H)Impact on confidentiality of information. High = complete loss of confidentiality.
Integrity (I)None (N), Low (L), High (H)Impact on integrity of information. High = complete loss of integrity.
Availability (A)None (N), Low (L), High (H)Impact on availability. High = complete loss of availability.

CVSS Vector Example

CVSS:3.0/AV:N/AC:L/PR:N/UI:N/S:U/C:H/I:N/A:N — Attack Vector Network, Complexity Low, No Privileges, No User Interaction, Scope Unchanged, Confidentiality High, Integrity None, Availability None. This represents a remotely exploitable vulnerability with no prerequisites that fully compromises confidentiality.

Know how to interpret a CVSS vector string. The exam may show a vector and ask you to identify what it means or rank vulnerabilities by severity. AV:N (Network) + PR:N (No privileges) + UI:N (No interaction) = worst possible combination = high score. If Impact = 0, base score = 0 regardless of other metrics.
CVSS Score Calculation
ISS (Impact Sub-Score)Calculated from Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability metrics. Represents the total impact on the target system. ISS = 1 - [(1-C) × (1-I) × (1-A)]. Impact ScoreDerived from ISS and Scope. If Scope=Unchanged: Impact = 6.42 × ISS. If Scope=Changed: Impact = 7.52 × [ISS - 0.029] - 3.25 × [ISS - 0.02]^15. Exploitability ScoreCalculated from Attack Vector, Complexity, Privileges Required, and User Interaction. Represents ease of exploitation. Base Score RuleIf Impact = 0, Base Score = 0. If Scope=Unchanged: Base = Impact + Exploitability. If Scope=Changed: Base = (Impact + Exploitability) × 1.08. Maximum score is 10. Rounded up to one decimal.
Vulnerability Validation and Context
True PositiveScanner correctly identifies a real vulnerability. The vulnerability exists and was correctly reported. Requires remediation. False PositiveScanner reports a vulnerability that doesn't actually exist. Wastes remediation resources. Reduce by using credentialed scans and keeping plugins updated. Always validate before remediating. True NegativeScanner correctly identifies no vulnerability. The system is not vulnerable and was correctly assessed. False NegativeScanner misses a real vulnerability. More dangerous than false positives — creates false sense of security. Reduce by using comprehensive scan configurations and credentialed scans. Context — InternalVulnerabilities on internal systems may be lower risk if the network is well-segmented and the system is not accessible from external networks or untrusted zones. Context — ExternalInternet-facing systems are highest priority. Any vulnerability that could be exploited by an unauthenticated remote attacker must be treated as critical. Context — IsolatedAir-gapped or highly isolated systems may have vulnerabilities that pose lower risk due to limited accessibility, even if the CVSS score is high. Asset ValueVulnerability severity must be considered in the context of what the vulnerable system does. A critical vulnerability on a non-critical test system may be lower priority than a medium vulnerability on a system storing sensitive PII. Exploitability/WeaponizationIs exploit code publicly available? Is it being actively exploited in the wild? CISA's Known Exploited Vulnerabilities (KEV) catalog identifies vulnerabilities actively exploited. Actively exploited = highest priority regardless of CVSS score. Zero-DayVulnerability with no patch available. No vendor-supplied fix exists. Organizations must rely on compensating controls, temporary mitigations, or accept the risk until a patch is released.
Common Vulnerability Types
Buffer OverflowWriting more data to a buffer than it can hold. Overwrites adjacent memory. Can corrupt data, crash programs, or allow attackers to execute arbitrary code. Types: stack overflow, heap overflow, integer overflow. Prevent: bounds checking, ASLR, DEP, safe functions. Privilege EscalationGaining access beyond what was authorized. Vertical = gaining higher privileges (user→admin). Horizontal = accessing another user's resources at the same level. Dirty COW is a famous kernel privilege escalation example. Arbitrary Code ExecutionAttacker can run code of their choice on the target system. Catastrophic if code runs with admin privileges. Often results from buffer overflows, injection flaws, or deserialization vulnerabilities. Missing PatchesUnpatched systems remain vulnerable to known exploits. Many major breaches exploit vulnerabilities patched months or years prior. Verizon DBIR found 4 overflow vulnerabilities (12–16 years old) responsible for 85% of exploited cases in their study. End-of-Life SystemsSystems where vendor no longer releases patches. Running EOL software = permanent unpatched vulnerabilities. Plan and budget for replacement before EOL dates. Insecure ProtocolsProtocols designed without security (Telnet, FTP, HTTP, older SSL/TLS, MD5, SHA-1). Should be replaced with secure equivalents (SSH, SFTP/SCP, HTTPS, TLS 1.2+, SHA-256+). Vulnerability scanners detect insecure protocol use. MisconfigurationSystems configured incorrectly in ways that allow attackers to gain information or access. Default credentials, unnecessary services, excessive permissions, verbose error messages, open cloud storage buckets. SQL InjectionInjects SQL commands into user input fields processed by a database. Can read, modify, or delete data. Prevent with parameterized queries/prepared statements. Cross-Site Scripting (XSS)Three forms: Persistent (stored in database, affects all visitors), Reflected (bounced off server in response), DOM-based (manipulates client-side JavaScript). Injects malicious scripts that execute in victims' browsers. Authentication VulnerabilitiesWeak passwords, lack of MFA, credential exposure, password reuse across sites. Password reuse enables credential stuffing attacks. Certificate IssuesCommon certificate problems: name mismatch (cert issued for different domain), expired certificate, unknown/untrusted CA (self-signed or internal CA not trusted by browsers), weak signature algorithm. IoT/Embedded SystemsOften have weak security — default passwords, no patch mechanism, long deployment lifecycles, limited computing resources. ICS/SCADA systems are particularly critical. Use network segmentation to isolate IoT from critical systems.

Risk Management Framework
Risk IdentificationIdentify threats and vulnerabilities in the operating environment. Combine to understand the risk landscape. Risk CalculationRisk Severity = Probability × Magnitude. Probability = likelihood the risk will occur. Magnitude = impact on the organization if it does occur. Risk AvoidanceStop the activity that creates the risk. Eliminate the risk by not doing the risky thing. Example: discontinue use of a vulnerable service entirely. Risk TransferenceTransfer financial consequences to a third party. Cyber insurance. SLAs with vendors. Does not eliminate the risk — only shifts financial exposure. Risk AcceptanceAcknowledge the risk and choose to continue operating without additional controls. Must be a documented, conscious decision — not ignoring the risk. Appropriate for low-probability, low-impact risks. Risk MitigationImplement controls to reduce probability or impact of the risk. The most common response. Examples: patching, adding MFA, network segmentation. ERM (Enterprise Risk Management)Formal organizational approach to risk analysis. Identifies risks, determines severity, adopts risk management strategies. Produces risk register documenting all identified risks and treatment decisions. BIA (Business Impact Analysis)Identifies critical business functions and the impact of disruptions. Determines RTO, RPO, and MTBF/MTTR for critical systems. Foundation of DR and BCP planning. Informs vulnerability prioritization — vulnerabilities affecting critical business functions get highest priority.
Security Controls
Managerial ControlsAdministrative and oversight controls. Policies, procedures, risk assessments, security planning. Set the framework within which technical and operational controls function. Operational ControlsImplemented by people rather than systems. Security awareness training, physical security, personnel security, incident response procedures. Technical ControlsImplemented by technology. Firewalls, encryption, MFA, IDS/IPS, SIEM, access control systems. PreventativeStop incidents before they occur. Firewalls, access controls, encryption, patching, security awareness training. DetectiveIdentify incidents that have occurred or are occurring. IDS, SIEM, audit logs, security cameras, anomaly detection. Responsive / CorrectiveRespond to and correct the impact of incidents. Incident response procedures, backups, forensics tools, patch management. Compensating ControlsAlternative security measures used when the primary control is not feasible. Must provide equivalent protection. PCI DSS specifically addresses compensating controls for merchants who cannot meet standard requirements.
Attack Surface Management
Attack SurfaceAll points where an attacker could interact with a system — ports, interfaces, APIs, user inputs, physical access points. Goal: minimize the attack surface. Remove everything that isn't needed. Adversary CapabilityAssessment of what the threat actor can do. Resources, skills, tools, access level. Higher capability actors require stronger defenses. Attack VectorsSpecific paths an attacker could use to exploit vulnerabilities. Network, web application, email, physical access, supply chain, insider threat. Edge DiscoveryIdentifying all externally facing assets. You can't protect what you don't know exists. Includes cloud assets, shadow IT, and legacy systems. Bug Bounty ProgramsInvite security researchers to find and responsibly disclose vulnerabilities in exchange for rewards. Supplements internal testing with external perspectives. Attack Surface ReductionDisabling unused services, ports, protocols, and features. Removing unnecessary software. Limiting internet exposure. Applying least privilege.
Change and Configuration Management
Configuration ManagementTracks how specific endpoint devices are set up. Maintains a known-good baseline for each system type. Enables detection of unauthorized changes (configuration drift). Change ManagementFormal process for identifying, requesting, approving, and implementing changes. Prevents unauthorized changes. Ensures changes are tested, documented, and reversible. Reduces risk of introducing new vulnerabilities. BaseliningSnapshot of a system or application at a known-good point in time. Compare current state against baseline to detect unauthorized changes. Used by FIM (File Integrity Monitoring) tools and SIEM correlation rules. Version ControlAssigns incrementing version numbers to software releases. Enables identification of what version is running on any system. Critical for vulnerability management — knowing exact versions allows accurate vulnerability matching. Patch ManagementProcess of identifying, testing, deploying, and verifying security patches. Test in non-production first. Deploy during maintenance windows. Validate successful application. Rollback plan for failed patches. WSUS for Windows environments. Yum/DNF/APT for Linux.
Secure Software Development Lifecycle (SDLC)
SDLC ModelsWaterfall: sequential phases (Requirements → Design → Implementation → Testing → Deployment). Spiral: iterative with risk management. Agile: short sprints with continuous delivery. RAD (Rapid Application Development): iterative prototyping, no planning phase. DevOps / DevSecOpsDevOps: combines development and IT operations to optimize the SDLC. DevSecOps: security integrated into DevOps — security is everyone's responsibility throughout the pipeline. "Shift left" means finding security issues earlier (cheaper) in development. CI/CDContinuous Integration: developers commit code to shared repository frequently with automated builds and tests. Continuous Deployment: tested changes automatically deployed to production. Risk: new vulnerabilities can be rapidly deployed. Logging and monitoring must fit the CI/CD model. Secure Coding Best PracticesInput validation (never trust user input). Output encoding (prevent injection). Session management (secure tokens, expiry, invalidation). Authentication (strong, MFA). Data protection (encrypt sensitive data at rest and in transit). Parameterized queries (prevent SQL injection). Code Testing TypesSAST (static — source code, no execution). DAST (dynamic — running app). Fuzzing (random/malformed input). Fault injection (deliberate errors). Mutation testing (intentional code bugs). Security regression testing (verify fixes don't reintroduce issues). Debuggers (Immunity Debugger, GDB).
Policies and Governance
PolicyHigh-level management statement of intent. Sets direction. Must be followed. Examples: Information Security Policy, Acceptable Use Policy (AUP), Data Classification Policy, Password Policy, Continuous Monitoring Policy. StandardsMandatory requirements describing HOW to comply with policy. More specific than policy. Approved at lower level than policies. Changes more regularly. ProceduresStep-by-step instructions for performing specific tasks. Most specific and detailed. Derived from standards and policies. GuidelinesRecommended practices — not mandatory. Advisory. Provide flexibility for complex situations. Exceptions and Compensating ControlsWhen normal controls cannot be applied, document the exception formally with business justification and implement compensating controls to mitigate the increased risk.

Attack Methodology Frameworks
Cyber Kill Chain (Lockheed Martin)Seven sequential phases of a targeted attack. Each phase must succeed for the attack to progress. Defenders can break the chain at any phase. Phases: Reconnaissance → Weaponization → Delivery → Exploitation → Installation → Command & Control → Actions on Objectives. Kill Chain — ReconnaissanceAttacker researches the target. OSINT, scanning, social engineering. Identifies targets, systems, and potential vulnerabilities. Kill Chain — WeaponizationCreates attack payload (malware, exploit) tailored to identified vulnerabilities. No target interaction yet. Kill Chain — DeliveryTransmits weapon to target. Email attachment, malicious website, USB drop, compromised update server. Kill Chain — ExploitationWeapon triggers, exploiting a vulnerability to execute code on the target system. Kill Chain — InstallationMalware installs on target. Backdoor, RAT, or persistent access mechanism established. Kill Chain — Command & Control (C2)Compromised system connects to attacker's C2 infrastructure. Attacker gains remote control. Beaconing behavior begins here. Kill Chain — Actions on ObjectivesAttacker achieves their goal: data exfiltration, lateral movement, ransomware deployment, sabotage, persistence.
Diamond ModelFour core features of every intrusion event: Adversary (attacker), Victim (target), Infrastructure (tools/systems used), Capability (TTPs employed). Connected by relationships forming a diamond shape. Used to discover more information by following the edges between events. Helps attribute attacks and understand attacker patterns. MITRE ATT&CKComprehensive knowledge base of real-world adversary tactics and techniques. Matrix format: Tactics (columns) = goals. Techniques (rows) = methods. Sub-techniques provide more specificity. Used for detection engineering, threat hunting, and purple teaming. OSSTMMOpen Source Security Testing Methodology Manual. Comprehensive testing methodology for security assessments. Provides rules of engagement, measurement metrics, and testing categories. OWASP Testing GuideComprehensive guide for testing web application security. Covers all OWASP Top 10 categories plus many additional tests. Provides specific test cases and techniques.
CSIRT Structure
CSIRTComputer Security Incident Response Team. Organizational function responsible for incident response. Has defined scope of authority, composition, and coordination mechanisms. CSIRT MembersTypically includes: IR analysts, forensic investigators, legal counsel, HR representative, PR/communications, management sponsor. Technical and non-technical roles both required. Scope of ControlDefines what systems and incidents the CSIRT has authority over. Larger scope = more complex coordination. Smaller scope = faster response within defined boundaries. CoordinationDuring incidents, the CSIRT coordinates with: system owners, law enforcement (if criminal activity), regulatory bodies (if required), insurance carriers, external forensics firms, and in major incidents, public relations.
Incident Classification
Functional ImpactDegree of impairment to the organization's ability to provide services. Ranges from no impact (business functions fully operational) to loss of a mission-critical function. Higher functional impact = higher severity. Economic ImpactFinancial consequences — direct losses, recovery costs, regulatory fines, reputational damage, lost business. Thresholds vary by organization size. Document and track throughout the incident. RecoverabilityTime and resources required to restore services to normal operation. Ranges from "regular" (fully recoverable with effort) to "non-recoverable" (permanent damage — notify leadership immediately). Information ImpactNature of data affected. Privacy data breach, intellectual property theft, unauthorized system access, or data destruction. Determines regulatory reporting requirements and notification obligations.
IR Phases
Preparation. Develop IR plan, build CSIRT, create playbooks, conduct tabletop exercises, acquire and test tools, train staff, establish BC/DR capabilities.
Detection and Analysis. Identify potential incidents from alerts, logs, public information, or people reports. Analyze to confirm incident and determine scope, impact, and severity.
Containment. Limit the spread and damage of the incident. Short-term containment (immediate isolation) and long-term containment (patching, rebuilding).
Eradication. Remove the root cause — malware, backdoors, unauthorized accounts. Patch vulnerabilities exploited. Rebuild compromised systems.
Recovery. Restore systems to normal operation. Validate that systems are clean and functioning. Monitor closely for signs of re-infection.
Post-Incident Activity. Root cause analysis, lessons learned review, update IR plan and playbooks, implement preventive measures, document findings in final report.
The exam uses NIST's IR phases. Know the order: Preparation → Detection/Analysis → Containment → Eradication → Recovery → Post-Incident. Preparation is not a reactive phase — it happens before incidents. Post-incident happens after. Some organizations merge Containment/Eradication/Recovery into one phase.
Preparation Phase Details
IR PlanDocumented policy and procedures for responding to incidents. Defines roles, responsibilities, communication channels, and escalation procedures. Must be reviewed and updated regularly. PlaybooksSpecific procedures for specific incident types (ransomware playbook, phishing playbook, data breach playbook). Developed during calm periods. Ready to execute when needed. SOAR can automate playbook execution. Tabletop ExerciseDiscussion-based scenario walkthrough. Key personnel talk through a simulated incident. No systems involved. Tests the plan and team coordination. Cheapest and lowest-risk form of exercise. IR Toolkit (NIST)At minimum: digital forensic workstations, backup devices, laptops for analysis, spare server/networking equipment, blank removable media, portable printer, forensic and packet capture software, bootable USB with trusted forensic tools, office supplies and evidence collection materials. BC/DRBusiness Continuity and Disaster Recovery planning ensures the organization can continue critical functions during and after major incidents. IR plan and BC/DR plan must be aligned.
Detection and Analysis Actions
  • Profile networks and systems — establish what "normal" looks like before you need to identify abnormal.
  • Understand normal behavior — behavioral baselines enable anomaly detection.
  • Create a logging policy — ensure sufficient logging is in place across all critical systems.
  • Perform event correlation — connect events across multiple sources to identify patterns indicative of incidents.
  • Synchronize clocks — NTP across all systems is critical for accurate log correlation and timeline reconstruction.
  • Maintain a knowledge base — document known threats, TTPs, and previous incidents to accelerate analysis.
  • Capture network traffic — full packet capture or NetFlow during suspected incidents enables forensic analysis.
  • Filter information — reduce noise to focus on high-priority signals.
  • Seek assistance — know when and how to engage external resources (threat intelligence services, CERT, law enforcement).

Indicators of Compromise (IoCs)
IoC DefinitionArtifacts, behaviors, or events that indicate a high probability that a system has been compromised. Used by security tools and analysts to detect active or past breaches. Three lenses: Collection, Analysis, Application. IoC — CollectionGathering data that may indicate compromise. Tools, logs, network monitoring, endpoint agents, threat intelligence feeds. Focus: acquiring sufficient data to perform meaningful analysis. IoC — AnalysisDetermining if collected data actually indicates compromise. Requires context — unusual traffic may be legitimate. Requires understanding of what the data means and whether it's likely benign or malicious. Threat intelligence helps provide context. IoC — ApplicationUsing confirmed IoCs to trigger incident response OR to build detection signatures for future monitoring. Threat intelligence services document and share IoCs for use in security monitoring tools.

IoC Feed Content Types

IP addresses and hostnames of malicious actors Domain names used by malware/C2 File hashes of malicious software Behavior-based indicators (TTPs) Email addresses used in attacks URL patterns associated with malware
IoC Categories
Network-Based IoCsUnusual outbound traffic destinations or volumes. Unexpected use of protocols (RDP, SSH, file transfers going outbound). DNS queries for unusual or flagged domains. Outbound traffic at unusual times. Traffic to/from known malicious IPs or C2 infrastructure. Host-Based IoCsProcessor/memory anomalies. New registry run keys or services. New user accounts created. Changes to scheduled tasks. Unexpected software or processes running. File system changes (new executables in unusual locations, file encryption activity). User/Account Behavior IoCsUnusual privileged account behaviors. Privilege escalation. Account activity at odd hours. Geographic impossibilities (login from New York and London 10 minutes apart — "impossible travel"). Addition of users to high-privilege groups. Bot-like activity patterns (precise timing, high volume, no variation). DNS-Based IoCsDNS queries for domains registered recently. Domains using DGA (Domain Generation Algorithms) — random-looking names. High-volume DNS queries to a single domain. DNS queries returning unusual response patterns (NXDOMAIN floods). File and Configuration IoCsModified system files. New files in sensitive directories (Windows system32, Linux /bin or /etc). Changes to critical configuration files. Log file tampering (evidence of an attacker covering tracks). DoS IoCsSudden extreme traffic volume increase. Service unresponsive or degraded. Traffic from many sources to a single destination. SYN flood signatures in network traffic.
Evidence Acquisition and Preservation
Chain of CustodyDocumentation tracking evidence through its entire lifecycle — who collected it, when, how, and where it has been since. Ensures evidence can be used in legal proceedings. Any break in chain of custody can render evidence inadmissible. Document every transfer, analysis, and storage event. Data IntegrityVerified using cryptographic hashing. Hash the evidence at collection, document the hash value, re-hash before analysis and compare. If hashes match, the evidence has not been altered. Common algorithms: MD5, SHA-1 (legacy), SHA-256 (current standard). PreservationObtaining, validating, and storing evidence needed for investigations or legal purposes. Preserve in write-protected, tamper-evident storage. Use write blockers when imaging drives. Never work on original evidence — always work on forensic copies. Legal HoldNotification issued during or before legal proceedings directing an organization to preserve data relevant to potential litigation. Requires preservation of all relevant data, even data scheduled for deletion per normal retention policies. Failure to comply can result in sanctions.
The exam expects you to understand all four evidence concepts: chain of custody (documentation of evidence handling), data integrity (hashing proves untampered), preservation (write-protect, forensic copies), and legal holds (must preserve everything when notified). All four are directly in the CS0-003 objectives.

Containment, Eradication & Recovery

Obj 3.2
Containment Strategies
Scope AssessmentBefore containing, determine the full extent of the compromise. Which systems are affected? What data may have been accessed or exfiltrated? How did the attacker get in? Containment without full scoping may leave backdoors in place. Impact AssessmentDetermine the business impact of the incident and the proposed containment actions. Containment itself can impact business operations — balance security need against operational impact. Network SegmentationProactive segmentation limits attacker lateral movement. During an incident, increase segmentation to isolate compromised segments from the rest of the network. ACLs and firewall rules can create emergency isolation without full disconnection. IsolationCompletely cut off compromised systems from the network while maintaining power for forensic investigation. More severe than segmentation — no network access at all. Preserves volatile data (RAM) while preventing further spread. RemovalTaking the system completely offline — power off or physically disconnect. Most severe containment. Destroys volatile data (RAM, running processes). Use only when isolation is insufficient or forensic analysis is complete. RemediationAddressing the vulnerabilities and configurations that allowed the breach. Patch, reconfigure, update, or replace affected systems and security controls. Compensating ControlsTemporary additional security measures deployed during containment when permanent fixes cannot be immediately implemented. Must be effective and documented.
Isolation vs. Removal: Isolation maintains the network connection but cuts it from the rest of the network (still on, still observable). Removal takes the system completely offline. Isolation is preferred for forensic purposes — keep systems powered on to preserve volatile evidence. Removal is a last resort.
Identifying Attackers
Attribution ChallengeIdentifying the perpetrators of a cybersecurity incident is complex and time-consuming. NIST guidance: "Identifying an attacking host can be a time-consuming and futile process that can prevent a team from achieving its primary goal — minimizing the business impact." Prioritize incident response over attribution during active incidents. Conflict with Law EnforcementOrganizations may want to notify law enforcement and preserve evidence for prosecution. But this can conflict with the need to eradicate and recover quickly. Establish legal counsel relationship and law enforcement contacts before incidents occur.
Eradication
Reconstruction / Re-imagingRebuild systems from scratch or restore from a known-good image. Most thorough eradication method. Eliminates all malware including sophisticated rootkits. Rebuild from the ground up or from a secure baseline image, not from backups that may be compromised. Root Cause in MindDuring rebuilding, address the root cause. Patch the vulnerability that was exploited. Remove the misconfiguration that allowed access. Otherwise the attacker will simply re-compromise the newly rebuilt system. PatchingApply all outstanding security patches before bringing systems back online. Prioritize patches for vulnerabilities involved in the incident. Sanitization and DisposalFor systems that cannot be returned to service or that stored highly sensitive data: overwrite (software), degauss (magnetic media), physical destruction (shredding, drilling). Choose method based on data sensitivity and media type. Document disposal for compliance.
Recovery and Post-Incident
Validating RecoveryBefore declaring recovery complete: verify systems are functioning normally, re-scan for vulnerabilities, monitor closely for signs of re-infection, confirm no backdoors remain, validate data integrity. Post-Incident ActivitiesRoot cause analysis determines what happened and why. Lessons learned identifies gaps and improvements. Final report documents the incident comprehensively. Update IR plan, playbooks, and security controls based on lessons learned. Final Report ComponentsChronology of events. Root cause. Location and description of evidence collected. Actions taken (containment, eradication, recovery) with rationale. Impact estimates. Results of post-recovery validation. Issues identified in lessons learned review.

Vulnerability Management Reporting
Report Critical ElementsListing of vulnerabilities. Affected hosts (hostname and IP). Risk score relevant to the organization. Mitigation options and recommendations. Notes on recurrence if vulnerability has appeared before. Prioritization information for remediation planning. Compliance ReportsReports demonstrating compliance with regulatory requirements (PCI DSS scan reports, FISMA assessments). Include evidence of controls in place, scan results, and any exceptions with compensating controls. Action PlansSteps to resolve identified vulnerabilities. Include: configuration management changes, patching schedule, compensating controls where patching isn't possible, awareness and training requirements, timeline and responsibility assignments. Management DashboardsHigh-level summary for leadership. Quick snapshot of security posture. Trends over time. Top 10 vulnerabilities. Critical and zero-day counts. SLO compliance status. Technical ReportsDetailed findings sorted by vulnerability type, severity, host group. Drill-down to specific host and vulnerability details including remediation guidance. Audience: security analysts and system administrators.
Stakeholders
Technical StakeholdersSystem administrators, network engineers, developers. Need detailed technical information to perform remediation. Want specific instructions and timelines. Security/Audit/ComplianceSecurity team, internal audit, compliance officers. Need comprehensive coverage data, exception documentation, and compliance status. Security ManagementCISO, security managers. Need metrics, trends, and risk summaries. Want to understand program effectiveness and resource allocation needs. Leadership/ExecutiveC-suite, board. Need business-risk framing, financial impact, and regulatory exposure. High-level metrics and summaries. Do not need technical detail.
Inhibitors to Remediation
MOU / SLAMemorandums of Understanding and Service Level Agreements may limit downtime or require advance notice before patching. Patch windows may be restricted. Work within contractual constraints while documenting the risk. Organizational GovernanceApproval processes, change advisory boards, and bureaucratic procedures slow down patching. Emergency change processes should exist for critical vulnerabilities. Business Process InterruptionPatching may require downtime that disrupts business operations. Schedule maintenance windows. For 24/7 operations, coordinate with business owners for minimal-impact windows. Degrading FunctionalitySome patches break existing functionality. Test patches in non-production environments first. Rollback plan essential. Legacy SystemsOlder systems may not have patches available. Vendor may no longer support them. Options: isolate, compensating controls, or replace. Document accepted risk formally. Proprietary SystemsVendor-controlled systems where the organization cannot apply patches. Must wait for vendor to release fixes. Use compensating controls. Engage vendor proactively about patch timelines.
Incident Response Communication
Stakeholder IdentificationIdentify who needs to know about the incident. Internal: executives, legal, HR, affected business units. External: regulators, customers, law enforcement, insurance carrier, public. Incident Declaration and EscalationFormal declaration that an incident has occurred. Triggers escalation procedures, notification requirements, and resource allocation. Defines the IR team's authority to act. Legal CounselMay or may not be involved in all incidents. Critical when: data breach with notification requirements, potential for litigation, law enforcement involvement, regulatory reporting. Communications with legal may be privileged. Public RelationsCustomer communication and media statements. Transparency builds trust. Premature or inaccurate communication can worsen damage. Have approved templates ready. Coordinate with legal before making any public statements. Regulatory ReportingMany regulations require breach notification within specific timeframes. GDPR: 72 hours. HIPAA: 60 days. PCI DSS: immediately notify card brands. Know your obligations before an incident occurs. Law EnforcementOrganization may engage law enforcement voluntarily or law enforcement may engage the organization. FBI, CISA, Secret Service (financial crimes) are common contacts. Engagement may affect investigation timeline and evidence handling.
Root Cause Analysis
Identify problems and events. Document all events that occurred as part of the incident as completely as possible.
Establish a timeline. Determine what happened and in what order. Helps identify root cause(s) and understand attacker progression.
Differentiate causes. Distinguish root causes from contributing factors and results. Identify which event is the actual root cause vs. what were consequences of it.
Document the RCA. Often presented as a diagram or chart. Used to communicate findings to stakeholders and drive remediation of root causes.
KPIs and Metrics
MTTD (Mean Time to Detect)Average time from the initial event that caused an incident to when it was detected. Requires forensic analysis to determine accurately. High MTTD indicates detection gaps. APT actors often achieve months or years of undetected access. MTTR (Mean Time to Respond)Time from detection to activating the incident response process (declaring the incident). Measures speed of triage and escalation. Different from time to remediate. Mean Time to RemediateMore complex to measure — each incident's size, scope, and complexity varies. Requires nuanced communication. May benefit from categorizing by incident type rather than a single aggregate number. Alert VolumeNumber of security alerts generated in a time period. High volume may indicate alert fatigue — analysts may miss real incidents. Work to reduce false positives and tune alert thresholds. Vulnerability MetricsTrends (improving or worsening over time?). Top 10 vulnerabilities by count or risk. Critical vulnerabilities and zero-days (immediate executive attention required). SLO compliance (are vulnerabilities being remediated within agreed timeframes?).
CH 13

Performing Forensic Analysis

Obj 3.2 · 3.3
Order of Volatility

Evidence must be collected in order of volatility — most volatile first (lost when power is removed) to least volatile. Failing to capture volatile evidence first means losing it forever.

OrderEvidence TypeVolatilityNotes
1CPU registers and cacheExtremely volatileLost immediately on shutdown. Rarely captured in practice.
2RAM / MemoryVery volatileLost on shutdown. Contains running processes, keys, passwords, network connections. Use memory acquisition tools (Volatility, WinPmem).
3Network connections and stateVery volatileActive connections, ARP cache, routing table. netstat, arp -a, route.
4Running processesVolatileList with ps aux or Task Manager before shutdown.
5Disk / StoragePersistentSurvives shutdown. Image with write blocker to preserve original.
6Logs (local)PersistentMay be tampered by attacker. Compare with centralized SIEM logs.
7Remote logging and monitoringMost persistentSIEM logs, cloud logs. Harder for attacker to tamper with.
8Physical configuration, network topology, archived mediaMost persistentHardware, network diagrams, offline backups.
Order of volatility is directly tested. Most volatile first: RAM → Network state → Running processes → Disk → Logs → Remote logs. Think about what data disappears when you pull the power cord. Never power off a system being investigated until volatile data is captured unless it's the only way to stop ongoing damage.
Forensic Investigation Process
Determine goals. What are you trying to find? What legal or business questions must be answered?
Outline locations and types of data. Where does relevant evidence reside? Which systems, which storage media, which logs?
Document and review plan. Get sign-off before beginning. Ensure legal authority exists. Understand scope and constraints.
Acquire and preserve evidence. Collect in order of volatility. Use write blockers. Hash everything. Document chain of custody.
Perform initial analysis. Review acquired evidence for obvious indicators. Build timeline. Identify key artifacts of interest.
Conduct deeper investigation. Follow leads from initial analysis. Correlate findings across evidence sources. Reconstruct attacker actions.
Report on findings. Document methodology, findings, and conclusions. Write for the intended audience (technical team, executives, legal, law enforcement).
Drive Image Acquisition and Validation
Write BlockersHardware or software device that prevents any writes to the original evidence drive. Ensures forensic acquisition does not alter the original evidence. Required for any forensic imaging. Hardware write blockers are more reliable than software. Forensic Drive DuplicatorsSpecialized hardware that creates exact bit-for-bit copies of storage media. Works independently without a computer. Creates multiple copies simultaneously. dd CommandLow-level Linux disk imaging. dd if=/dev/sda of=disk.img creates a bit-for-bit image. Slow. No verification built in — hash separately. dcfldd is a forensic-enhanced version with built-in hashing. Image VerificationHash the original drive and the forensic copy. Hashes must match to prove the copy is identical to the original. Use SHA-256 or MD5 (for legacy compatibility). Document both hash values in chain of custody documentation. Wiped Drives/MediaAlways use wiped, verified-empty media for evidence storage. Prevents contamination of evidence with data from previous investigations.
Log Acquisition and Analysis
Log Acquisition Steps1. Determine where logs reside and their format. 2. Determine the time period needed. 3. Obtain copy from system/device administrators and document how obtained. 4. Validate with checksums. 5. Identify items of interest (actions, user IDs, event IDs, timeframes). 6. Analyze with appropriate tools. Log Analysis ToolsSplunk — powerful SIEM and log analysis platform. ELA (Event Log Analyzer) — Windows event log analysis. Sawmill — multi-format log analysis. Text editors — for simple cases. grep, awk, sed — for CLI-based log analysis on Linux. Target Evidence LocationsSystem event logs (Windows/Linux). Application logs. Authentication/security logs. Network device logs (firewall, router, switch). Email server logs. Web server access logs. Database audit logs. Cloud provider logs (AWS CloudTrail, Azure Monitor).
Forensic Toolkit
Digital Forensic WorkstationDedicated, hardened workstation for forensic analysis. Isolated from production networks. Running forensic analysis software. Should be powerful enough to handle large image files and memory dumps. Forensic Investigation SuiteComprehensive software for evidence analysis. Examples: Autopsy (open source), FTK (Forensic Toolkit), EnCase. Provides file carving, keyword search, timeline analysis, artifact extraction, and reporting. Write BlockersHardware devices preventing writes to evidence media during acquisition. Critical for maintaining evidence integrity. Both IDE/SATA and USB blockers should be in the toolkit. Forensic Drive DuplicatorsHardware imagers creating verified forensic copies without a computer. Examples: Tableau, Logicube. Essential for high-volume forensics work. Supporting EquipmentWiped drives and removable media (verified empty). Various cables and adapters (SATA, IDE, USB, NVMe). Camera for documenting physical evidence. Evidence labels and bags. Notebooks for handwritten documentation. Portable printer for on-site documentation.
The NIST forensic toolkit contents are specifically listed in the CS0-003 objectives. Know: digital forensic workstation, forensic investigation suite, write blockers, forensic drive duplicators, wiped media, cables and adapters, camera, labels, and notebooks. These could appear in a scenario question asking what equipment to bring to an incident site.
Post-Incident Activity
Root Cause AnalysisDetermines what happened and why. Uses timeline analysis and evidence review. Identifies the initial access vector, attack progression, and enabling conditions. Drives remediation to prevent recurrence. Lessons LearnedStructured review of the incident response process itself. What worked? What didn't? What can be improved? Results in updates to IR plan, playbooks, detection rules, and security controls. Lessons Learned MeetingInclude all major stakeholders from the response. Blameless — focus on process improvement, not individual failure. Document all findings and action items. Assign owners and deadlines. Follow up to ensure improvements are implemented.
